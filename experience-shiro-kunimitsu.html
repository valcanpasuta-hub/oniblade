The Workshop

A Lineage Nine Hundred
Years in the Making

Nine hundred years ago, in the Heian period, a swordsmith named Miike Tenta lived in what is now Omuta, Fukuoka. His blades were considered the finest of his era — even Toyotomi Hideyoshi, the most powerful man in Japan, is said to have marvelled at their quality. The legend of Miike Tenta never left this town.

The Komiya family — the lineage behind Shiro Kunimitsu — took that legend as their standard. During the Edo period, they served as official swordsmiths (go-ban kaji) to the Yanagawa Domain, making blades on behalf of the feudal lord. In 1786, the family formally established the forge as a business. It has operated continuously since.

"In 1939, at the National Sword Test-Cutting Competition, the blade of Shiro Kunimitsu was pitted against Kotetsu — then considered the finest sword in Japan. The judge was Hakudo Nakayama, the era's most respected martial arts master. Shiro Kunimitsu won."

That same year, one of the forge's blades was selected among the three finest at the national exhibition and presented to the Emperor. The Navy Minister declared it without equal in Japan. The phrase the family adopted — "Kireaji ta ni rui nashi" — has never changed: "No equal in cutting performance."

When the Meiji government banned the wearing of swords in 1876, the Komiya family did not stop. They turned the same forge, the same hands, to kitchen knives and carpenter's tools — carrying the tradition forward through a different blade. Today, four swordsmiths continue that work in the same building in Omuta.

Experience 01

Make a Kitchen Knife with a Swordsmith

刀匠から教わる包丁制作

Five hours at the forge, guided by a swordsmith. You heat the steel in the furnace, hammer it into shape, quench it to set the hardness, grind and sharpen the edge, and stamp your name into the blade with a chisel — the same final act a swordsmith performs on every Japanese sword. The forge itself confirms: this process is almost identical, step for step, to how a Japanese sword is made.

Bring your own lunch. The knife you make goes home with you.

Experience 02

Forge Your Own Paper Knife

ペーパーナイフ作り

Under swordsmith guidance, you'll heat a five-inch nail in the forge, hammer it flat, and shape it into a ~15cm paper knife. A shorter, accessible introduction to what it actually feels like to work metal with fire. No experience required. No two will look the same.

Experience 03

Witness a Traditional Sword Forging

日本刀古式鍛錬

Watch the full ceremony of traditional Japanese sword forging — fire, hammer, steel. The heat of the furnace, the rhythm of the striker's hammer, sparks that fly in every direction. The swordsmith explains the blade's story directly, and you have the chance to take a hammer strike yourself. For groups of up to 50.

Forge viewing only (no forging demonstration): approx. A$33–A$44 / ~1 hour / up to 10 people.
